Who Are Sharks?

Sharks are cartilaginous fish known for their predatory skills and ancient lineage, existing for over 400 million years. They inhabit oceans worldwide and occupy various ecological niches, from reef predators to deep-sea hunters.

Sharks have keen senses including electroreception, excellent vision, and an acute sense of smell, making them efficient hunters.

Despite their fearsome reputation, most sharks are not dangerous to humans and play a crucial role in maintaining healthy ocean ecosystems.

Body Measurements & Size

Length: Most species 1–4 meters (3–13 ft), the largest, whale shark, up to 18–20 meters (59–66 ft).

Weight: Varies by species, giant sharks like the whale shark can weigh over 20 tons.

Teeth: Multiple rows of sharp teeth, adapted for grabbing, cutting, or filtering prey.

Fins & Tail: Powerful tail for propulsion, pectoral fins for stability and maneuvering.

Body: Streamlined for fast swimming and ambush hunting.

Hunting & Strength

Sharks are apex predators in many marine environments. They hunt using speed, stealth, and sensory adaptations to detect prey. Some species, like the great white, can ambush prey from below at high speed.

Whale sharks are filter feeders, consuming plankton, small fish, and krill, showing the diversity of feeding strategies among sharks.

Sharks maintain ocean health by controlling prey populations and removing sick or weak individuals, keeping ecosystems balanced.
