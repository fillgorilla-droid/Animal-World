Who Are Dolphins?

Dolphins are highly intelligent marine mammals belonging to the family of toothed whales. They are known for their agility, playful behavior, and complex social structures.

Dolphins live in pods, which are family groups that cooperate in hunting and protection. They use echolocation to locate prey and communicate using clicks, whistles, and body language.

While smaller than whales, dolphins are fast swimmers, reaching speeds up to 37 km/h (23 mph). They are important predators in the marine ecosystem, maintaining healthy fish populations.

Body Measurements & Size

Length: Typically 2–4 meters (6.5–13 ft), larger species up to 6 meters (20 ft).

Weight: Usually 150–650 kg, depending on species.

Dorsal Fin: Helps with stability during swimming; height varies by species.

Teeth: 80–100 conical teeth, adapted for catching fish and squid.

Flippers & Tail: Rounded flippers for maneuvering, strong tail for fast swimming.

Speed, Intelligence & Hunting

Dolphins are incredibly agile and intelligent. They hunt cooperatively, herding fish into tight balls for easier feeding. They can leap from the water to catch prey or evade predators.

Dolphins have been observed using tools, solving complex problems, and teaching hunting techniques to younger pod members.

Despite their smaller size compared to whales, dolphins are top predators in their environment and play a crucial role in marine ecosystems.
