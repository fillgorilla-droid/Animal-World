Who Are Killer Whales?

Killer whales, or orcas, are the largest members of the dolphin family. They are apex predators, hunting a variety of marine animals including fish, seals, and even whales.

Orcas live in complex social groups called pods, with strong family bonds and coordinated hunting strategies.

They communicate using a variety of clicks, whistles, and pulsed calls, each pod having unique dialects.

Body Measurements & Size

Length: Adult males up to 9 meters (30 ft), females up to 7 meters (23 ft).

Weight: Males up to 10 tons, females up to 7 tons.

Dorsal Fin: Males have a tall, triangular fin up to 2 meters high; females have shorter fins.

Teeth: 40–56 conical teeth used for hunting large prey.

Flippers: Large, rounded pectoral flippers for agile swimming and maneuvering.

Hunting & Strength

Killer whales are apex predators, hunting in coordinated pods using intelligence and teamwork.

They can take down large prey, including seals, sea lions, and even large whales, using speed, strength, and clever strategies.

Orcas can reach swimming speeds of up to 34 mph (55 km/h) in short bursts during hunting.

Despite being top predators, they have strong social structures and communicate effectively within pods.
