Who Are Gorillas?

Gorillas are the largest primates and among the strongest animals in the world. Adult male gorillas, also called silverbacks, can weigh up to 200 kg (440 lbs) and possess incredible upper body strength, capable of lifting and breaking objects many times their own weight.

Despite their immense strength, gorillas are generally gentle and social creatures. They live in groups called troops, led by a dominant silverback who protects and guides the family. Gorillas are mostly herbivorous, feeding on leaves, stems, fruits, and occasionally insects.

Their strong arms and chest allow them to climb, build nests, and defend against predators. Communication is complex, using vocalizations, gestures, and facial expressions. Gorillas play a key role in their ecosystem by dispersing seeds and maintaining forest health.

Gorilla Body Measurements & Strength

Height: Adult males can reach 1.7–1.8 m (5’7”–5’11”) standing upright.

Weight: 140–200 kg (310–440 lbs) for males, 70–90 kg (154–198 lbs) for females.

Arm Span: Up to 2.6 m (8’6”), giving incredible reach and climbing ability.

Hand & Foot Size: Hands ~24 cm long, Feet ~28 cm long, allowing strong grips and balance.

Chest & Waist: Chest circumference up to 160 cm, Waist ~100 cm, very muscular upper body.

Strength: Can lift, push, or break objects 6–10 times their body weight.

Gorillas use this strength for climbing, foraging, defending their troop, and building nests. Despite this, they are mostly gentle and social animals.

Gorilla Bite & Grip Strength

Bite Force: Up to 1,300 pounds per square inch (PSI), one of the strongest in mammals. This allows them to crush tough stems, fruits, and defend themselves if needed.

Grip Strength: Gorillas can exert over 400 lbs of force with their hands, making their grasp extremely powerful for climbing, holding branches, and manipulating objects.

Arm Power: Strong arms combined with long fingers give them incredible leverage for swinging and hanging.

Defense & Feeding: Bite and grip strength help in self-defense, feeding on fibrous plants, and building nests.

Despite this raw power, gorillas rarely use it aggressively; they are mostly gentle, social, and protective of their troop.
