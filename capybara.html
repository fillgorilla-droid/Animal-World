Who Are Capybaras?

Capybaras are the largest rodents in the world. Native to South America, they are semi-aquatic animals that live near rivers, lakes, and wetlands.

Despite their size, capybaras are gentle, calm, and very social. They live in groups that can include 10–30 individuals, led by a dominant male.

Capybaras are herbivores and spend most of their day eating grass, aquatic plants, and fruit. They are excellent swimmers and often rest in the water to stay cool and avoid predators.

Capybara Body Measurements

Length: 100–130 cm (3.3–4.3 ft).

Height: About 50–60 cm (20–24 in) at the shoulder.

Weight: 35–65 kg (77–143 lbs), making them the largest rodents on Earth.

Feet: Partially webbed feet help them swim efficiently.

Fur: Short, coarse, and water-resistant.

Build: Barrel-shaped body with short legs and a blunt snout.

Capybaras are built for life near water and can stay submerged for several minutes.

Teeth & Survival Adaptations

Teeth: Like all rodents, capybaras have large front incisors that never stop growing.

Bite Strength: Their strong teeth allow them to chew tough grasses and aquatic plants.

Swimming Ability: They can stay underwater for up to 5 minutes to escape predators.

Senses: Eyes, ears, and nostrils are located on top of the head, allowing them to stay mostly submerged while watching for danger.

These adaptations make capybaras excellent survivors in wetland environments.
