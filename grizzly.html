Who Are Grizzly Bears?

Grizzly bears (Ursus arctos horribilis) are large mammals found mainly in North America. They are known for their impressive size, strength, and brown fur with a characteristic grizzled appearance.

Grizzlies are omnivores, eating plants, berries, fish, and small mammals. They are solitary animals but may gather at food-rich areas like salmon streams.

Grizzly bears play a key role in their ecosystems by controlling prey populations and dispersing seeds through their diet.

Body Measurements & Size

Length: 2–2.8 meters (6.5–9 ft) from nose to tail.

Height: Up to 1.5 meters (5 ft) at the shoulder on all fours, 3 meters (10 ft) standing upright.

Weight: Males 270–680 kg (600–1500 lbs), females 135–200 kg (300–440 lbs).

Claws: 7–10 cm long, used for digging and hunting.

Grizzly bears are incredibly strong, capable of moving heavy objects and running up to 56 km/h (35 mph) over short distances.

Behavior & Strength

Grizzly bears are mostly solitary but communicate using vocalizations, scent marking, and body language.

They are excellent swimmers and climbers, and they hibernate in dens during winter, surviving on fat reserves.

Grizzlies are apex predators in their habitats, capable of taking down large prey and defending their territory effectively.
