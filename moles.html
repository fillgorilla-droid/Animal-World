Who Are Moles?

Moles are small burrowing mammals that spend most of their lives underground. They are found in many parts of the world, especially in forests, fields, and gardens.

Moles are solitary animals and rarely interact with others except during the breeding season. They create complex tunnel systems to search for food and stay safe from predators.

Unlike rodents, moles are insectivores. Their diet mainly consists of earthworms, insects, and other small invertebrates found in the soil.

Mole Body Measurements

Length: 11–16 cm (4.3–6.3 in) for most common species.

Weight: 70–130 g (2.5–4.6 oz).

Body Shape: Cylindrical body built for moving through tunnels.

Front Paws: Large, shovel-like paws used for digging.

Fur: Short, dense, and velvety, allowing movement in any direction underground.

Eyes: Very small and adapted for low-light environments.

Moles are perfectly adapted to life underground, where they dig tunnels and hunt for food.

Teeth & Underground Adaptations

Teeth: Sharp, pointed teeth designed for catching insects and worms.

Sense of Touch: Sensitive snout helps detect vibrations and movement in the soil.

Digging Ability: Powerful front legs allow them to dig tunnels quickly.

Hearing & Smell: Well-developed senses help them find prey underground.

These features make moles excellent hunters in dark, underground environments.
