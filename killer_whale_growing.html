0–1 Year: Calf Stage

Killer whale calves are born around 2.5 meters (8 ft) long and weigh 150–180 kg (330–400 lbs). They are fully dependent on their mothers’ milk for the first year and travel closely with their mothers and pod.

Calves learn basic social behaviors, pod structure, and communication sounds from older whales.

1–5 Years: Juvenile Stage

Between 1 and 5 years old, juveniles start eating solid food like fish and squid, learning hunting techniques from pod members. They grow rapidly and begin to participate in coordinated pod activities.

Social learning is crucial at this stage — calves memorize hunting strategies and communication calls.

5–15 Years: Subadult Stage

Subadults continue growing toward adult size: males up to 9 meters, females up to 7 meters. They refine hunting skills and become more independent while remaining within the pod.

Sexual maturity occurs around 10–15 years for females, later for males. Pod bonds and hunting skills remain vital for survival.

15+ Years: Adult Stage

Adult males can reach 9 meters and 10 tons, females up to 7 meters and 7 tons. Adults are fully integrated into the pod, leading hunts and maintaining social structure.

Killer whales can live 50–80 years in the wild. They continue learning and teaching hunting techniques, raising calves, and maintaining strong pod bonds.
